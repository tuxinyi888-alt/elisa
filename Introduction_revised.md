# Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (Mtb), most commonly affecting the lungs. It spreads through airborne droplets released when individuals with pulmonary TB cough, speak, or sneeze. According to the World Health Organization (2024), TB caused an estimated 10.7 million new cases and 1.23 million deaths in 2024, making it one of the leading infectious causes of mortality. Drug-susceptible TB is treated with six months of antibiotics, beginning with two months of isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide and ethambutol, followed by four months of isoniazid and rifampicin [1]. Effective treatment requires good adherence and monitoring for toxicity, and cases involving drug resistance often require longer regimens that include second-line drugs with higher toxicity. However, Mtb has a lipid-rich cell wall that limits antibiotic permeability and can persist for long periods within macrophages, allowing the infection to continue despite treatment [2]. In addition, the large global population with latent infection, together with the lengthy treatment course and increasing levels of drug resistance, indicates that antimicrobial therapy alone is insufficient for full disease control [3].

The course of TB is strongly influenced by the host immune response as well as by bacterial replication [4]. Excessive or dysregulated inflammation contributes to many of the pathological features of TB, driven by cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β and type I interferons, as well as sustained immune activation within granulomas [5]. This immunopathology contributes to lung tissue destruction, cavity formation and enhanced transmission, whereas insufficient immune responses fail to contain bacterial growth. Standard antibiotic therapy targets the pathogen but does not regulate harmful inflammation or protect host tissue [4]. These limitations have encouraged growing interest in host-directed therapy (HDT), which aims to modulate immune pathways to enhance antimicrobial activity while reducing immunopathology, and may be especially valuable in the context of drug-resistant disease [6].

Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are increasingly being regarded as promising candidates for HDT due to their potential for both immunomodulatory and antibacterial activity [7, 8]. Multiple *in vitro* and animal model studies have shown that various NSAIDs and their analogues, including ibuprofen and carprofen, can inhibit the growth of Mtb within achievable drug concentrations [9]. Some compounds also exhibit concentration-dependent bactericidal activity, even against certain drug-resistant strains. Mechanistic studies suggest that some NSAID analogs can interfere with key components of the Mtb electron transport chain, disrupting its energy metabolism and weakening cell membrane integrity [10]. Furthermore, studies have reported that these compounds can synergize with standard anti-tuberculosis drugs, providing a biological basis for using bactericidal NSAIDs as adjunctive therapy in existing chemotherapy regimens [11, 12].

In addition to their direct antimicrobial effects, NSAIDs may influence several aspects of the host immune response during Mtb infection. By inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes and altering the arachidonic acid–prostaglandin pathway, they can modify the local inflammatory environment and regulate cytokine production by macrophages and other immune cells [13]. NSAIDs may also modulate macrophage activation, bactericidal capacity and the balance between apoptosis and necrosis through effects on NF-κB signaling, inflammasome activity and the generation of reactive oxygen and nitrogen species. These changes have the potential to influence bacterial clearance, antigen presentation and the quality of T cell responses. NSAIDs with microbicidal activity therefore represent promising candidates for therapeutic strategies that target both the pathogen and the host immune response [7, 8, 13].

Despite these observations, the specific immunological effects of microbicidal NSAIDs in the context of mycobacterial infection remain poorly defined. In particular, it is not yet clear how such compounds influence macrophage responses to innate stimuli or during mycobacterial infection itself, or whether they can restrict intracellular bacterial growth within macrophages. Addressing these gaps is essential to determine whether microbicidal NSAIDs can be used as effective adjuncts to TB treatment.

To investigate these questions, this project uses carprofen as a representative microbicidal NSAID and *Mycobacterium bovis* bacillus Calmette–Guérin (BCG) as a biosafety level 2 model for Mtb, examining its immunomodulatory effects *in vitro* using human monocyte-derived macrophages (MDMs). The specific objectives are:

1. To evaluate the effects of carprofen on MDM innate immune responses to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and BCG stimulation.
2. To assess the effects of transient and sustained carprofen treatment on intracellular and extracellular BCG growth in MDMs.

# Results

## Cytokine production in LPS-stimulated MDMs co-treated with carprofen

To investigate whether carprofen modulates the pro-inflammatory cytokine response to bacterial stimulation, MDMs from four healthy donors were stimulated with lipopolysaccharide (LPS; 0, 1, 10, and 100 ng/mL) in the presence of increasing concentrations of carprofen (C0, C1, C10, and C100 µg/mL) for 6 hours. Supernatants were collected and IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α levels were measured by sandwich ELISA. Data were analysed by two-way ANOVA with carprofen concentration and LPS concentration as factors.

In the absence of LPS (L0), IL-1β was undetectable across all carprofen conditions. LPS stimulation induced low but measurable IL-1β production. Two-way ANOVA revealed significant main effects of both LPS concentration (F = 7.543, p = 0.0003) and carprofen concentration (F = 3.247, p = 0.030), as well as a significant interaction between the two factors (F = 2.615, p = 0.015). At the highest carprofen concentration (C100 µg/mL), IL-1β levels increased with escalating LPS doses, reaching 11.16 ± 7.38 pg/mL at C100 + L100, compared with 2.43 ± 1.68 pg/mL at C0 + L100 (Figure X). This suggests a synergistic effect between high-dose carprofen and LPS on IL-1β release.

LPS stimulation induced a robust, dose-dependent increase in IL-6 production. At L100, mean IL-6 ranged from 326.61 to 390.53 pg/mL regardless of carprofen condition. Two-way ANOVA confirmed a highly significant effect of LPS concentration (F = 28.240, p < 0.0001), but no significant effect of carprofen (F = 0.161, p = 0.922) and no interaction (F = 0.355, p = 0.951) (Figure X).

TNF-α was the most abundantly produced cytokine, with levels reaching 7389.85 ± 2428.48 pg/mL at C0 + L100. A dose-dependent increase in TNF-α with LPS concentration was confirmed by two-way ANOVA (F = 13.867, p < 0.0001). Neither the main effect of carprofen (F = 1.294, p = 0.287) nor the interaction term (F = 0.391, p = 0.934) was significant. A non-significant trend towards reduced TNF-α at C100 µg/mL was noted (e.g. 3916.76 ± 4884.86 pg/mL at C100 + L100 versus 7389.85 ± 2428.48 pg/mL at C0 + L100), but high inter-donor variability precluded statistical significance (Figure X).

## Cytokine production in BCG-stimulated MDMs co-treated with carprofen

To assess the effect of carprofen on the immune response to mycobacterial challenge, MDMs were co-treated with carprofen (C0, C1, C10, and C100 µg/mL) and BCG (MOI 0, 1, 10, and 100) for 6 hours.

BCG stimulation induced considerably lower cytokine responses compared to LPS across all three analytes. IL-1β remained largely undetectable, with sporadic low-level production at higher BCG doses (maximum: 10.83 ± 14.35 pg/mL at C10 + B100). Two-way ANOVA showed no significant effects of carprofen (F = 0.954, p = 0.422), BCG concentration (F = 1.271, p = 0.295), or their interaction (F = 1.089, p = 0.388) (Figure X).

IL-6 levels following BCG stimulation were markedly lower than those observed with LPS, remaining below 20 pg/mL even at the highest BCG dose. Neither carprofen (F = 0.916, p = 0.440) nor BCG concentration (F = 2.633, p = 0.061) reached significance, though the latter showed a borderline trend towards dose-dependent induction (Figure X).

BCG elicited a modest TNF-α response with a trend towards higher levels at increasing MOIs (maximum: 412.83 ± 730.58 pg/mL at C10 + B100), though inter-donor variability was substantial. Two-way ANOVA confirmed no significant effect of carprofen (F = 0.356, p = 0.785), BCG concentration (F = 2.491, p = 0.071), or their interaction (F = 0.713, p = 0.694) (Figure X).

## BCG growth assay by flow cytometry

To determine whether carprofen can directly restrict mycobacterial growth within macrophages, a BCG growth assay was performed under two treatment conditions: transient exposure, in which carprofen was present only during the initial 4-hour infection period before being replaced with drug-free media, and sustained exposure, in which carprofen was maintained continuously in the culture medium for the full 7-day assay period. This design allowed us to distinguish between the immediate bactericidal effects of carprofen and the requirement for prolonged drug exposure to control intracellular mycobacterial replication.

### Uninfected controls (B0)

In the absence of BCG infection, fold change values remained close to 1.0 across both treatment conditions and both the extracellular (EC) and intracellular (IC) fractions (EC media only: 1.12–1.64; EC +carprofen: 0.69–1.64; IC media only: 0.94–1.34; IC +carprofen: 0.96–1.24), confirming that neither carprofen treatment nor the 7-day culture period introduced significant changes in background fluorescence.

### Extracellular BCG

In the media only group, extracellular BCG showed a clear dose-dependent reduction in growth at MOI B10, with fold change decreasing from 4.47 ± 1.58 (C0) to 3.01 ± 1.32 (C1), 2.47 ± 0.87 (C10), and 1.30 ± 0.17 (C100 µg/mL) (mean ± SEM, n = 3). This approximately 3.4-fold reduction indicates that even transient exposure to carprofen during the initial 4 hours was sufficient to inhibit subsequent extracellular BCG replication in a concentration-dependent manner. A similar trend was observed at MOI B100 (C0: 11.28 ± 8.02 to C100: 4.05 ± 1.59), although inter-donor variability was substantial at this MOI. At MOI B1, fold change values were modest (1.18–1.64) with no clear dose-dependent pattern.

In the +carprofen group, extracellular BCG fold change at MOI B10 was lower overall compared to the media only group (range: 1.55–2.49), and the dose-dependent gradient was attenuated. At MOI B100, continuous carprofen treatment similarly reduced fold change relative to the media only group (C0: 6.02 ± 2.67 vs 11.28 ± 8.02), with the highest concentration again producing the lowest fold change (C100: 1.96 ± 0.42). These results suggest that sustained carprofen exposure further suppresses extracellular BCG growth beyond the effect of initial treatment alone.

### Intracellular BCG

In the media only group, intracellular BCG fold change at MOI B10 showed a trend opposite to the extracellular fraction: higher carprofen concentrations during initial treatment were associated with increased intracellular fold change (C0: 1.14 ± 0.25 to C100: 2.63 ± 0.85). This may indicate that transient carprofen exposure, while restricting extracellular growth, does not inhibit — and may modestly enhance — intracellular BCG replication over the 7-day period.

In the +carprofen group, this pattern was reversed. At MOI B10, sustained carprofen treatment at C100 µg/mL markedly reduced intracellular BCG fold change to 0.68 ± 0.04, compared to 2.63 ± 0.85 in the media only group. A similar inhibitory effect was observed at MOI B100, where fold change in the +carprofen group was consistently lower across all concentrations (range: 0.44–1.04) relative to the media only group (range: 1.07–1.67). These findings indicate that continuous carprofen treatment is required to effectively restrict intracellular BCG replication.

## ELISA findings

Carprofen did not significantly alter IL-6 or TNF-α production in response to either LPS or BCG stimulation. However, a significant synergistic effect between carprofen and LPS on IL-1β release was observed, with the highest carprofen concentration (C100 µg/mL) potentiating LPS-induced IL-1β production. A non-significant trend towards reduced TNF-α at C100 µg/mL was noted in LPS-stimulated MDMs, but high inter-donor variability precluded statistical significance. BCG stimulation induced considerably lower cytokine responses compared to LPS across all three analytes, and carprofen had no significant effect on BCG-induced cytokine production, possibly reflecting the weaker innate immune activation by BCG at the 6-hour timepoint.

## BCG growth findings

Taken together, these data demonstrate that carprofen exerts concentration-dependent inhibitory effects on BCG growth. Transient carprofen exposure (4 hours) was sufficient to inhibit extracellular BCG replication, particularly at MOI B10, but did not restrict intracellular growth. In contrast, sustained carprofen treatment over 7 days was necessary to suppress intracellular BCG replication. The differential response between extracellular and intracellular compartments likely reflects differences in drug accessibility: carprofen may readily access extracellular bacteria even during brief exposure, whereas sustained intracellular drug concentrations are required to inhibit phagosomal BCG. These results highlight the importance of continuous drug exposure for effective control of intracellular mycobacterial infection.
