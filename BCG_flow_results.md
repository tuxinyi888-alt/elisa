# Results: Effect of Carprofen on BCG Growth

## BCG growth assay by flow cytometry

To assess the effect of carprofen on mycobacterial growth, macrophages were infected with BCG-dsRed at three multiplicities of infection (MOI: B1, B10, B100) in the presence of increasing concentrations of carprofen (0, 1, 10, 100 µM). Two treatment conditions were compared: in the first group (media only, n = 3 donors), carprofen was present only during the initial 4-hour infection period and replaced with media alone after washing; in the second group (+carprofen, n = 3 donors), the corresponding carprofen concentration was re-added after the 4-hour wash and maintained for the duration of the 7-day culture. BCG growth was quantified by flow cytometry at 4 hours post-infection (4 hpi, baseline) and 7 days post-infection (7 dpi). Integrated fluorescence (IF) was calculated as the product of BCG event counts and the geometric mean PE-A fluorescence intensity, normalised to bead counts. Fold change in IF (7 dpi / 4 hpi) served as the primary readout of BCG replication. Uninfected controls (B0) were included to account for background fluorescence.

### Uninfected controls (B0)

In the absence of BCG infection, fold change values remained close to 1.0 across both treatment conditions and both the extracellular (EC) and intracellular (IC) fractions (EC media only: 1.12–1.64; EC +carprofen: 0.69–1.64; IC media only: 0.94–1.34; IC +carprofen: 0.96–1.24), confirming that neither carprofen treatment nor the 7-day culture period introduced significant changes in background fluorescence.

### Extracellular BCG

In the media only group, extracellular BCG showed a clear dose-dependent reduction in growth at MOI B10, with fold change decreasing from 4.47 ± 1.58 (C0) to 3.01 ± 1.32 (C1), 2.47 ± 0.87 (C10), and 1.30 ± 0.17 (C100) (mean ± SEM, n = 3). This approximately 3.4-fold reduction indicates that even transient exposure to carprofen during the initial 4 hours was sufficient to inhibit subsequent extracellular BCG replication in a concentration-dependent manner. A similar trend was observed at MOI B100 (C0: 11.28 ± 8.02 to C100: 4.05 ± 1.59), although inter-donor variability was substantial at this MOI. At MOI B1, fold change values were modest (1.18–1.64) with no clear dose-dependent pattern.

In the +carprofen group, extracellular BCG fold change at MOI B10 was lower overall compared to the media only group (range: 1.55–2.49), and the dose-dependent gradient was attenuated. At MOI B100, continuous carprofen treatment similarly reduced fold change relative to the media only group (C0: 6.02 ± 2.67 vs 11.28 ± 8.02), with the highest concentration again producing the lowest fold change (C100: 1.96 ± 0.42). These results suggest that sustained carprofen exposure further suppresses extracellular BCG growth beyond the effect of initial treatment alone.

### Intracellular BCG

In the media only group, intracellular BCG fold change at MOI B10 showed a trend opposite to the extracellular fraction: higher carprofen concentrations during initial treatment were associated with increased intracellular fold change (C0: 1.14 ± 0.25 to C100: 2.63 ± 0.85). This may indicate that transient carprofen exposure, while restricting extracellular growth, does not inhibit — and may modestly enhance — intracellular BCG replication over the 7-day period.

In the +carprofen group, this pattern was reversed. At MOI B10, sustained carprofen treatment at C100 markedly reduced intracellular BCG fold change to 0.68 ± 0.04, compared to 2.63 ± 0.85 in the media only group. A similar inhibitory effect was observed at MOI B100, where fold change in the +carprofen group was consistently lower across all concentrations (range: 0.44–1.04) relative to the media only group (range: 1.07–1.67). These findings indicate that continuous carprofen treatment is required to effectively restrict intracellular BCG replication.

### Summary

Taken together, these data demonstrate that carprofen exerts concentration-dependent inhibitory effects on BCG growth. Transient carprofen exposure (4 hours) was sufficient to inhibit extracellular BCG replication, particularly at MOI B10, but did not restrict intracellular growth. In contrast, sustained carprofen treatment over 7 days was necessary to suppress intracellular BCG replication. The differential response between extracellular and intracellular compartments likely reflects differences in drug accessibility: carprofen may readily access extracellular bacteria even during brief exposure, whereas sustained intracellular drug concentrations are required to inhibit phagosomal BCG. These results highlight the importance of continuous drug exposure for effective control of intracellular mycobacterial infection.
