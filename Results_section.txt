## Results

### Effect of carprofen on cytokine production in LPS-stimulated PBMCs

To evaluate the immunomodulatory potential of carprofen in the context of bacterial stimulation, PBMCs from four healthy donors were treated with varying concentrations of carprofen (0, 1, 10, and 100 μM) in combination with LPS (0, 1, 10, and 100 ng/ml) for 6 hours. The levels of three pro-inflammatory cytokines — IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α — were quantified by sandwich ELISA.

**IL-1β.** In the absence of LPS stimulation (L0), IL-1β was undetectable across all carprofen concentrations. LPS stimulation induced measurable IL-1β production in a dose-dependent manner; however, the overall levels remained low (mean range: 1.93–11.16 pg/ml). Two-way ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of LPS concentration (F = 7.543, p = 0.0003) and carprofen concentration (F = 3.247, p = 0.030), as well as a significant interaction between the two factors (F = 2.615, p = 0.015). Notably, at the highest carprofen concentration (100 μM), IL-1β levels appeared to increase with escalating LPS doses, reaching a mean of 11.16 ± 7.38 pg/ml at C100 + L100, compared with 2.43 ± 1.68 pg/ml at C0 + L100. This suggests a potential synergistic effect of high-dose carprofen and LPS on IL-1β production.

**IL-6.** LPS stimulation induced robust IL-6 production, with levels increasing in a clear dose-dependent manner. At the highest LPS dose (L100), mean IL-6 levels ranged from 326.61 to 390.53 pg/ml across carprofen conditions. Two-way ANOVA demonstrated a highly significant main effect of LPS concentration (F = 28.240, p < 0.0001), but no significant effect of carprofen (F = 0.161, p = 0.922) and no significant interaction (F = 0.355, p = 0.951). These findings indicate that carprofen did not significantly modulate LPS-induced IL-6 production at any of the concentrations tested.

**TNF-α.** TNF-α was the most abundantly produced cytokine following LPS stimulation. Mean TNF-α levels reached 7389.85 ± 2428.48 pg/ml at C0 + L100 and 3916.76 ± 4884.86 pg/ml at C100 + L100. Two-way ANOVA revealed a highly significant main effect of LPS concentration (F = 13.867, p < 0.0001), but no significant effect of carprofen (F = 1.294, p = 0.287) and no significant interaction (F = 0.391, p = 0.934). Although a trend towards reduced TNF-α production was observed at the highest carprofen dose (C100) relative to the untreated control (C0) across multiple LPS concentrations, this effect did not reach statistical significance, likely due to the high inter-donor variability.

### Effect of carprofen on cytokine production in BCG-stimulated PBMCs

To assess whether carprofen modulates the immune response to mycobacterial stimulation, PBMCs were co-treated with carprofen (0, 1, 10, and 100 μM) and BCG at varying multiplicities of infection (MOI: 0, 1, 10, and 100) for 6 hours.

**IL-1β.** BCG stimulation induced minimal IL-1β production overall. Detectable levels were observed mainly at higher BCG doses (B10 and B100), but values remained low and highly variable across donors. The highest mean was observed at C10 + B100 (10.83 ± 14.35 pg/ml). Two-way ANOVA showed no significant main effects of carprofen (F = 0.954, p = 0.422) or BCG concentration (F = 1.271, p = 0.295), and no significant interaction (F = 1.089, p = 0.388).

**IL-6.** BCG-induced IL-6 production was markedly lower compared with LPS stimulation. At the highest BCG dose (B100), mean IL-6 levels remained below 20 pg/ml across all carprofen conditions, compared with >300 pg/ml observed under equivalent LPS conditions. Two-way ANOVA revealed no significant effects of carprofen (F = 0.916, p = 0.440), BCG concentration (F = 2.633, p = 0.061), or their interaction (F = 0.549, p = 0.831). The borderline significance for BCG concentration (p = 0.061) suggests a trend towards dose-dependent IL-6 induction by BCG, but this did not reach the conventional threshold for statistical significance.

**TNF-α.** BCG stimulation elicited a modest TNF-α response, with a general trend towards increased production at higher BCG MOIs. The highest mean TNF-α level was observed at C10 + B100 (412.83 ± 730.58 pg/ml), though with substantial inter-donor variability. Two-way ANOVA showed no significant main effects of carprofen (F = 0.356, p = 0.785) or BCG concentration (F = 2.491, p = 0.071), and no significant interaction (F = 0.713, p = 0.694). Similar to IL-6, the BCG concentration effect approached borderline significance (p = 0.071), indicating a trend that may become significant with a larger sample size.

### Summary

Across all three cytokines, LPS stimulation induced a robust, dose-dependent pro-inflammatory response that was largely unaffected by carprofen co-treatment. The exception was IL-1β, where a significant interaction between carprofen and LPS concentration was observed, suggesting a potential modulatory role of carprofen at high concentrations. In contrast, BCG stimulation induced comparatively modest cytokine responses after the 6-hour infection period, and no statistically significant effects of carprofen were detected. The high inter-donor variability, particularly evident in TNF-α measurements, may have limited the statistical power to detect subtle effects. These results suggest that carprofen does not broadly suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine production in response to either LPS or BCG stimulation in human PBMCs under the conditions tested.
