## Results: PGE2 production in LPS- and BCG-stimulated MDMs co-treated with carprofen

To investigate whether carprofen modulates prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) production, supernatants from the same 6-hour co-treatment experiments were analysed by competitive ELISA. PGE2 concentrations were interpolated from four-parameter logistic (4PL) standard curves fitted to duplicate standards (7.8–1000 pg/mL; Plate 2 R² = 0.992, Plate 3 R² = 0.999). Data were analysed by two-way ANOVA with carprofen concentration and stimulus dose as factors (n = 4 donors).

### PGE2 in LPS-stimulated MDMs

In the absence of carprofen (C0), LPS stimulation produced a dose-dependent trend towards increased PGE2 production, rising from 96.06 ± 5.53 pg/mL at L0 to 199.47 ± 22.80 pg/mL at L100 (mean ± SEM), though the main effect of LPS did not reach statistical significance (F = 2.642, p = 0.060). Two-way ANOVA revealed a highly significant main effect of carprofen concentration (F = 22.036, p < 0.0001), as well as a significant carprofen × LPS interaction (F = 3.311, p = 0.003).

At intermediate carprofen concentrations (C1 and C10), the LPS-induced increase in PGE2 was abolished. Mean PGE2 at C1 remained between 82.09 and 91.95 pg/mL across all LPS doses, and at C10 between 86.02 and 96.43 pg/mL, with no dose-dependent LPS response (Figure 5A). This is consistent with COX inhibition by carprofen blocking stimulus-dependent PGE2 synthesis.

At the highest carprofen concentration (C100), baseline PGE2 in the absence of LPS was elevated relative to C0 (140.01 ± 6.01 vs 96.06 ± 5.53 pg/mL), but the LPS dose-dependent increase was completely abolished (C100 + L100: 125.41 ± 12.69 pg/mL). This elevated baseline at C100 was consistently observed across all four donors and may reflect cytotoxicity-mediated release of pre-formed PGE2 or compensatory upregulation of COX-2 expression at supratherapeutic drug concentrations, rather than de novo COX-dependent synthesis.

### PGE2 in BCG-stimulated MDMs

BCG stimulation did not significantly alter PGE2 levels at any MOI (F = 1.187, p = 0.325). In the absence of carprofen, mean PGE2 ranged from 57.58 pg/mL (B1) to 98.77 pg/mL (B100), with substantial inter-donor variability. A significant main effect of carprofen was observed (F = 5.658, p = 0.002), but there was no significant carprofen × BCG interaction (F = 0.736, p = 0.674) (Figure 5B).

At C100, baseline PGE2 was again elevated (119.48 ± 23.51 pg/mL at B0), mirroring the pattern observed with LPS. At C1 and C10, PGE2 levels were modestly reduced compared to C0 across BCG doses, consistent with partial COX inhibition, though these differences were smaller than in the LPS condition. The absence of a significant BCG-induced PGE2 response is consistent with the generally weak cytokine responses observed with BCG at the 6-hour timepoint.


## Discussion additions (to integrate into existing Discussion)

### Insert after the paragraph on IL-1β potentiation (paragraph 1 of Discussion):

The observed potentiation of LPS-induced IL-1β at high carprofen concentrations was accompanied by a corresponding alteration in PGE2 production. Carprofen at 1 and 10 µg/mL effectively blocked the LPS-induced increase in PGE2 (from ~96 to ~200 pg/mL at C0 to a flat ~85–96 pg/mL), with a significant carprofen × LPS interaction (p = 0.003). This is consistent with the known mechanism of carprofen as a COX inhibitor and provides direct evidence that carprofen suppresses stimulus-dependent prostaglandin synthesis in this experimental system. Since PGE2 has been shown to inhibit NLRP3 inflammasome activation through EP2/EP4 receptor signalling and intracellular cAMP elevation [17, 18], the carprofen-mediated reduction in PGE2 may contribute to the observed potentiation of IL-1β production by relieving this tonic inhibitory signal. These findings support a mechanistic link between COX inhibition, PGE2 suppression, and enhanced inflammasome-dependent IL-1β release.

Notably, at the highest carprofen concentration (100 µg/mL), baseline PGE2 was paradoxically elevated compared to the untreated control, despite the complete abolition of LPS-induced PGE2 accumulation. This observation was consistent across all four donors and may reflect non-enzymatic release of pre-formed PGE2 from damaged cell membranes at this supratherapeutic concentration, or a compensatory upregulation of COX-2 transcription. This finding parallels the non-significant trend towards reduced TNF-α at C100, which together may indicate cytotoxic effects at this concentration that could confound interpretation of the immunomodulatory data.

### Insert into the BCG discussion paragraph:

PGE2 production was not significantly affected by BCG stimulation at the 6-hour timepoint, consistent with the weak cytokine responses observed. A significant main effect of carprofen on PGE2 was detected (p = 0.002), but the absence of a BCG × carprofen interaction suggests that carprofen modulates basal rather than BCG-induced PGE2 production at this early timepoint. Longer stimulation periods (24–72 hours) may be required to capture mycobacterially-induced prostaglandin responses.

### Insert into the Limitations paragraph:

Additionally, PGE2 was measured at only one timepoint (6 hours), which may not capture the full kinetics of prostaglandin production, particularly in response to mycobacterial stimulation. The elevated PGE2 at C100 µg/mL across both LPS and BCG conditions raises concerns about cytotoxicity at this concentration; future studies should include a cell viability assay (e.g. LDH release) to distinguish genuine immunomodulation from cytotoxic artefacts.

## Materials and Methods addition (add to "Cytokine quantification by ELISA" section):

PGE2 was measured in the same supernatants using a competitive ELISA kit (Cayman Chemical) according to manufacturer instructions. Absorbance was read at 405 nm on a Synergy H1 plate reader (BioTek). PGE2 concentrations were determined by interpolation from four-parameter logistic (4PL) standard curves fitted to duplicate standards (range 7.8–1000 pg/mL) using non-linear least squares regression in R. Percentage bound (%B/B0) was calculated as ((sample OD – NSB mean) / (B0 mean – NSB mean)) × 100, where NSB is the non-specific binding control and B0 is the maximum binding control.
